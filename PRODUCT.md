# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

Two audiences, equally primary: passengers and driver-partners in Astana and
across Kazakhstan, arriving on their phones to decide whether to download the
Sooncar taxi app. Passengers are evaluating whether the price and experience
beat their current ride-hailing habit; driver-partners are evaluating whether
the commission and earnings are worth switching to. Neither audience is
secondary — the page must never let one dominate the other visually or in
copy. Role (passenger vs. driver) is chosen inside the app after install;
there is no separate driver app or signup flow.

## Product Purpose

A single-page marketing/download landing for Sooncar, a taxi/ride-hailing app
serving Astana and other Kazakhstan cities. Its only job is converting a
visitor — passenger or driver-partner — into an app install via the App Store
or Google Play, while surfacing the product's real differentiators: fair
upfront pricing, low driver commission, trust signals (ratings, live
tracking), intercity coverage, and local-language (Kazakh) support. Success
is measured in installs from either audience through the store links; there
is no other conversion path on the page.

## Positioning

The taxi app that's honest to both sides of the ride — fair pricing for
passengers and low commission for drivers, not one funded by the other.

## Conversion & proof

- Primary CTA: download the app (App Store or Google Play) — the only ask on
  the page, repeated identically in the hero and the final CTA.
- Secondary CTA: none. This is a single-minded download page; there is no
  contact form, newsletter, or "learn more" fallback. Footer contact details
  exist for support, not as a marketing fallback path.
- The line a visitor should remember after 10 seconds: "Такси, в котором
  выгодно и пассажиру, и водителю" — benefit cuts both ways.
- Belief ladder, in order: (1) the price is fair and known before the ride,
  no surprise markups; (2) driver commission is genuinely low, so more of
  each fare reaches the driver; (3) ratings and live tracking make the ride
  trustworthy for both sides; (4) intercity routes mean one app covers more
  of a rider's or driver's trips; (5) it's built in Kazakhstan with real
  Kazakh-language support, not a foreign product with a translation bolted
  on.
- Proof on hand: none yet (no testimonials, press, download counts, or
  partner logos currently featured). This is a future opportunity, not a gap
  to fake — do not invent placeholder proof.

## Brand Personality

Refined minimalism, zero-friction. Restraint is the point, not an absence of
effort — trust and speed are meant to read as competence, not as visual
noise. The page should feel precise and uncluttered rather than loud;
distinctive means quiet confidence, not maximalism, playfulness, or
editorial flourish.

## Anti-references

Bold/maximalist, brutalist, retro-futuristic, or editorial-magazine
treatments. Playful or toy-like visual language. Purple/indigo gradient
hero fills. Generic SaaS blue-on-white. Any pattern that reads as "AI landing
page" scaffolding (tracked-uppercase eyebrows, numbered section markers,
identical card grids, hero-metric stat blocks).

## Design Principles

- Equal weight for both audiences — passenger and driver-partner messaging
  are sized, ordered, and styled identically everywhere they appear together.
- Follow established ride-hailing UX patterns (Jakob's Law) instead of
  inventing novel interactions for a category users already know.
- Restraint signals trust — competence is communicated through precision and
  speed, not decoration; every section should earn its place or be cut.
- Single-minded conversion — every section moves toward the one action
  (download); no competing asks, no dead scroll that doesn't inform or move
  toward that action.
- Local credibility is a real differentiator — Kazakhstan-built and
  Kazakh-language support is a proof point to state plainly, not an
  afterthought or a footnote.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum contrast on all text/background pairs actually used.
Semantic HTML landmarks (`header`, `main`, labeled `section`, `footer`).
Alt text on all screenshots — they're evidentiary product proof, not
decoration. Minimum touch target 48×48px (the larger of iOS HIG's 44px and
Material's 48dp). Keyboard-navigable with visible focus states on all store
buttons. Motion is CSS-only, minimal (page-load reveal + CTA hover), and
should not block or delay access to the primary CTA.
